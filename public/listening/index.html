Opening

The library opens on a Sunday
in lockdown.

On March 22nd, 2020, one thousand and forty-four songs arrive in a single day — a migration, not a beginning. The archive that existed before this date is unknowable, locked in older accounts and other formats. What follows is everything the software remembers: six years and change of saving, skipping, returning.

From that opening week, the pace slows to something sustainable — an average of 38 songs a month, steadily, for the next six years. No month is ever empty. The collection grows the way a garden does: new things added, old things quietly tended, rarely uprooted. Average popularity of a track in this library is 41 of 100 — not mainstream, not willfully obscure. Curious.

Chapter I · The Weather

A taste that refuses
a single aesthetic.

The top-line answer is rock — in every strain Spotify knows how to name: art, psychedelic, alternative, classic, progressive, garage, blues. But rock is only part of the story. Beneath it runs a persistent second current of Bollywood, Sufi, and Hindi pop — and a third of nu-metal, indie folk, and electronic. This is a library that doesn't pretend to one aesthetic.

Chapter II · The Eras

Eight decades live in this library.

The 2010s dominate — that's when the saving started, and Spotify's catalog is richest there. But 513 tracks come from before the year 2000, including 150 from the 1970s alone. The past isn't decoration here. It's lived in.

The 1970s show up through Pink Floyd (60 tracks saved) and Porcupine Tree's later homage work. The 1990s come in through grunge and post-grunge — Nirvana, Oasis, alt-rock. The 2020s are where the present arrives — 650 tracks and counting, arriving at a rate of roughly a song every two days.
